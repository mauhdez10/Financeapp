**Good to know**

- A 0% balance transfer only helps if you stop adding new charges and pay the debt off before the promotional period ends — write the end date on your calendar the day the card arrives.
- With a secured card your deposit is refundable. Pick one with no annual fee that reports to all three credit bureaus, pay on time, and you can usually upgrade to a regular card in 6-12 months.
- You never have to pay anyone to get a credit card or to 'fix' your credit first — anyone charging an upfront fee for that is a red flag. Comparison sites like NerdWallet and Bankrate are free; they earn a commission if you apply through their links, which doesn't cost you anything.
- Before applying anywhere, check your credit score for free (your bank app or AnnualCreditReport.com) so you apply for cards that match your score — every denied application is a small hit to your credit.

- No Social Security number or no credit history does not mean no loan. Lenders like Lendbuzz accept an ITIN and look at your income instead — you do not have to settle for a high-interest 'buy here, pay here' lot.
- If you already have a car loan above roughly 8-9%, ask a credit union about refinancing. If your credit has improved since you bought the car, refinancing can lower your payment with no fee to apply.

- Government rent help never costs money to apply. If anyone asks for a fee to 'process' your Section 8 or rental-assistance application, it is a scam — apply only through your local housing authority or 211.

**Good to know**

- Applying for government benefits is always free. If a website or caller asks for a fee to "process" your SNAP, LIHEAP, or Medicaid application, it is a scam — hang up and use the official .gov site instead.

- You always apply through your STATE, not the federal site. The federal pages just point you to your state's portal (in Florida it's MyACCESS). Make sure the address ends in .gov before entering personal information.
- If the website feels confusing, call instead: dial 211 for local help in English or Spanish, call 1-877-543-7669 for kids' health coverage, or visit your county benefits office in person — phone and in-person applications count the same as online ones.

- Before paying any large hospital bill, do two things: ask for an itemized bill (errors are common) and ask about the hospital's financial assistance or 'charity care' program — nonprofit hospitals are required by law to have one.

- Freezing your credit is free at all three bureaus (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion) and stops thieves from opening accounts in your name. It takes minutes by phone or online, and you can unfreeze it anytime for free.
- Sign up for Medicare during the 7-month window around your 65th birthday — missing it can mean a penalty added to your premium for life. A free SHIP counselor can walk you through it in person, in English or Spanish.

- New baby: the clock starts at birth. You typically have only 30 days to add the baby to job-based health insurance (60 days on HealthCare.gov). Call HR or the Marketplace the same week, not at tax time.
- Divorce: the divorce decree does NOT remove your name from joint credit cards, the mortgage, or the car loan — creditors can still come after you. Close or refinance joint accounts, open one small account in your own name, and check all three credit reports free at AnnualCreditReport.com.
- Widowhood: never pay anyone to 'find' survivor benefits or lost life insurance — Social Security and the NAIC policy locator are free. Also report the death to Social Security right away (the funeral home often does it), and ask about the one-time $255 lump-sum death payment.

If the family truly can't pay, ask the county or city about its indigent burial/cremation assistance program before signing a funeral contract.

- Your home or renters insurance does NOT cover flooding — that needs a separate flood policy, and there is usually a 30-day wait before it kicks in. Buy it before hurricane season, not when a storm is on the map.
- After a storm: take photos of ALL damage before cleaning anything up, then report your claim to your insurer immediately. Florida law gives you as little as one year to file, and insurers treat late claims with suspicion.
- Never sign paperwork from a contractor who knocks on your door after a storm. 'Assignment of Benefits' contracts are illegal in Florida — if anyone asks you to sign over your insurance rights or 'direct payment,' call the state helpline first (1-877-693-5236) and verify the license.
- If FEMA denies you, that is usually NOT the final answer — most denials are missing documents. You have 60 days to appeal, and the appeal letter can be written in Spanish.
